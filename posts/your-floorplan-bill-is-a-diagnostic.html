May 9, 2026 Diagnostics

Your Floorplan Bill Is a Diagnostic

Most dealer groups treat inventory as a volume game. The ones protecting their bottom line in 2026 treat it as a high-velocity asset. Here is what the data shows.

The monthly floorplan bill is not a fixed cost of doing business. It is a diagnostic report on operational inefficiency. Most dealer group owners and GMs treat inventory as a volume game, yet the data is clear: the groups protecting their bottom line right now treat it as a high-velocity asset. Carrying too much inventory is a slow-motion margin leak, and most groups have never quantified it at the unit level.

The numbers are worth sitting with. As of early 2026, the industry average for new-vehicle inventory has reached 76 days' supply, with used inventory sitting at 39 days. Those figures might look normal compared to the inventory-starved years of the pandemic, but the cost of carrying that stock has fundamentally shifted. Floorplan holding costs have escalated to $7.90 per day per unit, up 14% from prior-cycle norms driven by sustained interest rate pressure.

A 10-day reduction in days' supply across a 1,000-unit inventory is a direct $79,000 monthly contribution to net profit.

At current holding costs of $7.90/unit/day

That is not a rounding error. That is a margin decision hiding inside an inventory report most ownership groups review once a month, if at all.

Top-performing groups are not operating on the same math as the industry average. The elite decile of retailers, particularly those anchored by high-throughput brands like Toyota and Lexus, are executing a disciplined approach called Variable Management. These groups run at a 28 to 35 day supply for new vehicles and under 30 days for used units. By prioritizing velocity over volume, they have reduced daily holding costs to a range of $3.50 to $4.50 per unit. They are moving 154 new units per store monthly. The industry average is 67. The difference is not brand demand alone. It is a fundamental rejection of the volume-at-all-costs mindset in favor of unit-specific profitability.

Two operational mandates separate high-velocity groups from everyone else. First, they have moved aggressively away from the auction. While average dealers still source nearly 30% of their used inventory from auctions, top-tier groups have placed a hard 15% cap on auction-sourced stock. They replace that volume with street-purchase programs and service drive acquisitions, which protect gross margins and insulate the group from the depreciation risk that auction-heavy sourcing creates.

Second, they have professionalized their merchandising speed. Top performers run a 60-minute-to-web protocol. Trade-ins are washed, photographed, and listed within one hour of acquisition, often as "Just Traded" or "Coming Soon" to generate early demand before the unit hits the active lot. A one-day delay in getting a vehicle online is a $7.90 penalty against the eventual gross on that unit, compounded across every day it sits unmarketed. These groups also use 20 Group data to restructure their physical lots, pushing halo vehicles to high-traffic rows and stocking aggressively toward the third-row SUVs and minivans that dominate demand in truck-and-SUV-heavy markets like Colorado and the Mountain West.

The gap between the groups executing on this and those waiting for the next market cycle to bail them out is widening. High performers are using predictive analytics to flag aging risk by VIN before a unit ever becomes a problem, adjusting pricing proactively rather than reactively. They have connected their digital retailing data to the showroom floor so that what a buyer sees online matches what they are presented with in person, which eliminates the friction points that erode trust and kill deals.

Ownership needs to stop leading with unit volume and start asking about the velocity of the capital sitting on the asphalt.

Three questions every GM should be able to answer cold

  1. What is the exact daily holding cost of our current inventory?
  2. What percentage of our used units were sourced from auction this month?
  3. How many units have been on the lot for more than 45 days?

If the answer to any of those is "I'll have to get back to you," the margin is already walking out the door. At $7.90 a day, per unit, it adds up fast.
